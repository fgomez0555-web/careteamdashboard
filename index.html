Prevention & Services
Since 2018, West Hollywood has invested approximately $8 million per year — about 5% of the City's operating budget — in a coordinated system of services, which includes services to address and prevent homelessness. That system has helped 411 people find permanent housing, placed 781 individuals in interim shelter, and provided over 1,800 households with rental assistance to prevent homelessness. The initiative is now evolving with a new Coordinated Response Framework — building on what's working to serve the community even better.
How the system works
Proactive outreach and a coordinated path — from first contact to a permanent home
1
Outreach teams go out
Trained outreach teams engage people experiencing homelessness throughout West Hollywood — building trust, offering services, and meeting people where they are. Community members can also call the Homeless Concern Line (323-848-6590) to request help.
2
Needs are assessed
Teams engage with individuals, assess their health, housing, and service needs, and connect them with the right support — from medical care and meals to case management and benefits.
3
Shelter when ready
When someone is ready for housing, they can enter an interim housing placement. The Holloway Interim Housing Program serves West Hollywood community members experiencing chronic homelessness. Other interim and emergency shelter options are available for individuals who do not meet that criteria.
4
A permanent home
Case managers work with each person to secure permanent housing, apply for benefits, and access the ongoing support they need to stay housed long-term.
What the City funds
The City funds services across six core areas
📢
Street outreach & crisis response
Trained teams engage people where they are — providing care, connecting them to services, and responding to behavioral health crises 24/7.
🏠
Interim & emergency shelter
Short-term housing placements for individuals working toward a permanent home. The Holloway Interim Housing Program serves West Hollywood community members experiencing chronic homelessness. Additional interim and emergency shelter options are available for those who do not meet that criteria, including transitional housing for youth.
🔒
Permanent housing placement
Case managers connect individuals to permanent supportive housing, rental vouchers, and long-term stability — 411 people housed since 2016.
💼
Rental assistance & eviction prevention
Financial help for households at risk of losing their housing — including emergency rental assistance for older adults and eviction defense.
🏥
Health & recovery services
Medical care, behavioral health support, substance use recovery, and services for people living with HIV/AIDS — helping individuals stabilize and rebuild.
⚖️
Legal services & tenant rights
Free legal help to prevent evictions, protect tenant rights, and navigate benefits — keeping vulnerable households from falling into homelessness.
What's next
Building on success: the Coordinated Response Framework
West Hollywood's Homeless Initiative has delivered real results — 411 people permanently housed, the Holloway Interim Housing Program — West Hollywood's first in-city interim housing facility, serving community members experiencing chronic homelessness — and the Homeless Concern Line now operating 24/7 with expanded coverage managed by the Care Team. The City is now developing a Coordinated Response Framework (CRF) to build on this progress. The CRF will strengthen coordination across outreach teams, interim housing providers, housing programs, and public safety partners — ensuring people move more smoothly through the system and that the City's investment delivers the greatest possible impact.
The Homeless Concern Line (323-848-6590) is West Hollywood's non-emergency number for homelessness-related concerns. Anyone — residents, visitors, City staff, or businesses — can call to request outreach or services for someone they are concerned about. Calls are answered 24/7 and routed to a trained response team.

What prevention services cover
The City funds four types of prevention and stability services
Rental assistance
Emergency financial help for households at risk of losing their housing — including dedicated assistance for community members 55 and older.
Eviction defense & tenant rights
Free legal help to fight evictions, understand tenant rights, and access housing benefits — keeping people in their homes.
Health & recovery support
Benefits counseling, substance use recovery, and services for people living with HIV/AIDS — addressing the underlying factors that can lead to housing instability.
Outreach to at-risk communities
Targeted outreach and peer support for transgender, gender non-conforming, and intersex community members who face heightened risk of housing instability.
Key takeaways
A coordinated, multi-layered systemWest Hollywood's approach connects outreach teams, interim housing providers, housing programs, and public safety partners to move people from first contact into permanent stability — with 411 individuals permanently housed since 2016.
HCL is the front door993 requests routed in nine months, with 73% of teams arriving within 15 minutes. The line is growing — recent months averaging 6–7 requests per day as community awareness increases.
Housing capacity is expandingInterim housing admissions grew from 64 in 2021 to 151 in 2025. The Holloway program added 20 in-city beds in October 2025 for West Hollywood community members experiencing chronic homelessness, reaching full occupancy within weeks.
Prevention keeps people housedOver $2.6 million in rental assistance has been disbursed since 2018. Eviction defense and tenant advocacy add an additional layer of stability for at-risk households.
Locally collected Point-in-Time data — 2017 to 2025
West Hollywood’s contracted outreach providers have conducted monthly counts since 2017, supplementing the annual LAHSA count with more frequent, West Hollywood–specific observations of unsheltered homelessness. Counts are conducted during early morning hours on a consistent schedule. The average monthly count grew from approximately 30 individuals in 2017 to approximately 55 in 2025 — consistent with broader regional trends across Los Angeles County.
